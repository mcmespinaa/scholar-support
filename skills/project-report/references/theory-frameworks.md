## 1. Causal Layered Analysis (CLA) / Iceberg Model

**What it is:** A four-layer analytical framework that moves from surface symptoms to deep structural causes.

**Four layers:**
- **Litany (Symptoms):** Observable events and trends (e.g., declining sales, reduced market share)
- **Systems/Social Causes:** Institutional structures, policies, economic dynamics that produce symptoms
- **Worldview/Discourse:** Dominant assumptions and framings that sustain the systems (e.g., food as commodity)
- **Myths/Metaphors:** Deep cultural narratives and mental models (e.g., "cheap food is a right")

**When to use:** Section 1 (problem analysis) and Section 5 (theoretical reflection). Demonstrates depth of problem understanding.

**Key citation:** Inayatullah, S. (2004). The Causal Layered Analysis reader.

## 2. Theory of Change

**What it is:** A methodology for planning, participation, and evaluation that maps the causal logic from activities to long-term impact.

**Structure:**
- **Inputs/Activities:** What the initiative does
- **Outputs:** Direct products of activities (measurable)
- **Outcomes:** Changes in behaviour, norms, or capacity (medium-term)
- **Impact:** Long-term systemic change

**Non-linear version:** For complex adaptive systems, ToC acknowledges feedback loops, emergent outcomes, and non-linear pathways rather than simple cause-effect chains.

**When to use:** Section 3 (sustainability impact) and Section 4 (impact measurement).

**Key citation:** Kickul & Lyons (2025), pp. 88-89.

## 3. Systems Thinking & System Leadership

**What it is:** An approach that views problems as embedded in interconnected systems with feedback loops, rather than isolated linear causes.

**Key concepts:**
- **Nested systems:** Individual actions are shaped by higher-level structures
- **Feedback loops:** Reinforcing (amplifying) and balancing (stabilising) dynamics
- **Adaptive spaces:** Zones where new ideas can interact with existing structures
- **Three capabilities of system leaders** (Senge et al., 2015): See the larger system, foster reflection, shift from reactive to generative conversation

**When to use:** Section 2 (concept rationale), Section 5 (theoretical reflection).

**Key citations:**
- Senge, P., Hamilton, H., & Kania, J. (2015). The dawn of system leadership. Stanford Social Innovation Review.
- Meadows, D. (2008). Thinking in Systems.

## 4. Sustainable Business Model Canvas

**What it is:** An extension of the traditional Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010) that adds sustainability dimensions.

**Standard 9 blocks + 3 sustainability extensions:**
- Value Propositions, Customer Segments, Key Partners, Key Activities, Key Resources, Channels, Customer Relationships, Revenue Streams, Cost Structure
- Social Sustainability, Environmental Sustainability, Economic Sustainability

**When to use:** Part A (canvas), Section 2 (business model description).

**Key citation:** Evans, S., et al. (2017). Business model innovation for sustainability. Business Strategy and the Environment.

## 5. Communities of Practice (CoP)

**What it is:** Groups of people who share a concern or passion and learn together through regular interaction.

**Three elements:**
- **Domain:** Shared area of interest
- **Community:** Joint activities, discussions, relationship-building
- **Practice:** Shared resources, tools, methods, stories

**When to use:** Section 2 (if initiative involves community-building), Section 3 (knowledge exchange outcomes).

**Key citation:** Wenger, E. (1998). Communities of Practice: Learning, Meaning, and Identity.

---

## 6. Transformative Social Innovation

**What it is:** Social innovation that challenges, alters, or replaces dominant institutional structures.

**Key concepts:**
- **Empowerment and disempowerment:** TSI can simultaneously empower some actors while disempowering others
- **Institutional change across scales:** Micro, meso, macro levels
- **Resistance and struggle:** Transformative change involves political contestation

**When to use:** Section 1 (understanding why change is hard), Section 5 (theoretical reflection on power dynamics).

**Key citations:**
- Avelino, F., et al. (2019). Transformative social innovation and (dis)empowerment. Technological Forecasting and Social Change.
- Haxeltine, A., et al. (2013). Transformative social innovation: A sustainability transitions perspective (TRANSIT working paper).

## 7. Multi-Level Perspective (MLP)

**What it is:** A framework for understanding socio-technical transitions through three interacting levels.

**Three levels:**
- **Niche:** Protected spaces where radical innovations develop
- **Regime:** Established practices, rules, and technologies (the dominant system)
- **Landscape:** Exogenous factors (macro-trends, crises, cultural shifts)

**When to use:** Section 1 (niche-regime dynamics), Section 3 (transition pathways).

**Key citation:** Geels, F. (2002). Technological transitions as evolutionary reconfiguration processes.

---

## 8. Institutional Entrepreneurship

**What it is:** How actors create, transform, or disrupt institutions to advance new practices.

**Key insight:** The obstacle is often not a lack of product or service, but a lack of institutional infrastructure (trust, knowledge, norms) to support adoption.

**When to use:** Section 2 (how the initiative redesigns market practices), Section 5 (theoretical reflection).

**Key citation:** Kickul & Lyons (2025), Ch. 2-3.

---

## 9. Doughnut Economics

**What it is:** An economic framework proposing that economies should operate within ecological boundaries (ceiling) while ensuring social foundations (floor).

**When to use:** Section 1 (framing systemic economic failures), Section 3 (planetary boundaries context).

**Key citation:** Raworth, K. (2017). Why it's time for doughnut economics. IPPR Progressive Review.

---

## 10. Problem Tree Analysis

**What it is:** A visual tool that maps a core problem, its causes (roots), and effects (branches).

**Structure:**
- **Trunk:** Core problem statement
- **Roots:** Direct causes, underlying causes, root causes
- **Branches:** Direct effects, wider effects, systemic consequences

**When to use:** Section 1 (problem analysis), as complement to CLA.
