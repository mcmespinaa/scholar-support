# Research Proposal Skill (Research Methods)

## Overview

This skill produces research proposals as `.docx` files for individual written assignments in research methods courses (e.g., OL652E at Malmö University). The proposal must be 2,000 words (+/- 10%, excluding diagrams, figures, and references), connect to a UN Sustainable Development Goal (SDG), and address leadership and organisation for sustainability. The skill is adaptable to any research methods course with similar assignment structures.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when the user asks you to:
- Write or draft a research proposal for a research methods course
- Revise or improve an existing research proposal draft
- Structure a research idea into the required proposal format
- Get help with any section of the research proposal (background, methods, etc.)
- Develop research questions for both qualitative and quantitative perspectives

## Assignment Requirements

**Format:** 1 document, 2,000 words +/- 10% (1,800–2,200 words body text, excluding diagrams, figures, and references)

**Grading scale:** A (Excellent), B (Very Good), C (Good), D (Satisfactory), E (Pass), U (Fail)

**Core requirement:** Use one of the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and choose a topic within leadership and organisation for sustainability.

## Proposal Structure

### a) Background and Problem Discussion (approx. 500–750 words)

This section establishes the research territory and identifies the gap or problem.

**Required elements:**
1. **Actualizing the research area**
   - "So what?" — Why is this an important area to study empirically?
   - Connect to real-world urgency (policy, practice, societal trends)
   - Link to relevant SDG(s)

2. **Literature and theory positioning**
   - What leadership, organisation, and sustainability literature and theories will be used, and why?
   - Begin a **problematization** of the research area based on current literature
   - Use problematization over gap-spotting where possible (per Sandberg & Alvesson, 2011 — a key course reading)

3. **Research problem**
   - What potential contribution can the research make to the existing body of knowledge?
   - What societal values will the research potentially address?

4. **Purpose statement**
   - Clear, concise statement of the research aim
   - Should logically follow from the problem discussion

**Writing guidance:**
- Use a funnel structure: broad context → specific problem → purpose
- Follow "setting the hook" principles (Grant & Pollock, 2011): open with a compelling framing that signals why the reader should care
- Integrate at least 8–12 citations in this section
- Balance empirical evidence with theoretical framing

### b) Previous Studies (approx. 500 words)

A focused review of 4–6 relevant empirical articles.

**Required elements:**
- Review each article in terms of:
  - Research questions
  - Scope of study
  - Methods (aim for a balance of quantitative and qualitative studies)
  - Data used
  - Results/findings
- Explain how your proposed research can **use / build on / support / differ from** these studies

**Writing guidance:**
- Do not just summarise — synthesise and compare across studies
- Organise thematically or methodologically, not article-by-article
- Show awareness of both methodological traditions (qual and quant)
- Identify what the studies collectively leave unaddressed (this feeds into your contribution)
- Use a mini review matrix to keep track:

| Author(s) & Year | RQ / Aim | Method | Key Findings | Relation to Your Study |
|-------------------|----------|--------|--------------|----------------------|

### c) Methods and Research Questions (approx. 500 words)

Dual-perspective methodological design covering both qualitative and quantitative approaches.

**Required elements:**

1. **Qualitative perspective:**
   - Two tentative research questions (must align with purpose)
   - Brief explanation of potential data gathering method (e.g., semi-structured interviews, focus groups, document analysis)
   - Brief explanation of potential data analysis method (e.g., thematic analysis, content analysis, narrative analysis)

2. **Quantitative perspective:**
   - Two tentative research questions (must align with purpose)
   - Brief explanation of potential data gathering method (e.g., survey, secondary data, experimental design)
   - Brief explanation of potential data analysis method (e.g., regression, descriptive statistics, SEM)

3. **Research plan illustration:**
   - A brief timeline or process diagram showing how you would manage the research process
   - Based on the research process model from the introductory lecture
   - Gustav's guidance: "basically I want you to reflect a bit (very briefly) how the process would materialize (time management and consider it a way to start thinking about how to create a plan to finalize the thesis on time)"
   - This is a minor part of the grade — keep it concise

**Writing guidance for research questions:**
- Qualitative RQs typically start with "How" or "What" and explore experiences, perceptions, meanings
- Quantitative RQs typically ask about relationships, differences, effects, and include measurable variables
- All four RQs must clearly align with the stated purpose
- Reference Agee (2009) and Creswell for research question formulation principles

### d) Practical Contribution and Stakeholder Perspective (approx. 250–500 words)

**Required elements:**
- To whom could the research be interesting and relevant? (practitioners, policymakers, organisations, communities)
- How can the knowledge generated be useful for them?

**Writing guidance:**
- Be specific about stakeholder groups
- Connect back to the SDG and societal values mentioned in section (a)
- Consider multiple levels: organisational, sectoral, policy, community
- Avoid vague claims — describe concrete ways findings could be applied

### e) Reference List

- APA 7 format
- All sources cited in the text must appear here
- Minimum ~15–20 references for a strong proposal
- Include a mix of: foundational theories, recent empirical studies, methodological sources, policy/institutional sources

## Assessment Criteria

The grading criteria are adopted from Hug & Aeschbach (2020) and emphasise:

| Criterion | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Originality** | Is the research idea novel and engaging with the research frontier? |
| **Academic & Extra-Academic Relevance** | Scientific value AND societal value |
| **Plausibility & Soundness** | Coherence, justifiability, appropriateness of the design |
| **Background ↔ Questions link** | Clear and appropriate connection between problem and RQs |
| **Methods ↔ Questions link** | Clear and appropriate fit between methods and RQs |
| **Quality of Description** | Clarity and completeness of writing |
| **Feasibility** | Awareness of resources needed (timeline, data access, skills) |

### Grade Targets

| Grade | Key Requirements |
|-------|-----------------|
| **A** | Original idea engaging the research frontier. Excellent academic + societal relevance. Exceptional flow between background → purpose → RQs. Excellent theory–question–method alignment. Near-flawless text. Supersedes all B criteria. |
| **B** | Original idea, well-explained relevance. Well-illustrated theory–question–method relevance. Very well-structured. Plus all C criteria. |
| **C** | Clear link between background/problem and purpose/questions. Feasible research plan. Fair understanding of RQ formulation, methods, data gathering, and analysis. Relevant literature cited. All sections present and well-structured. |
| **D–E** | Relevant, clear idea. Key sections present. Demonstrates course learning goals achieved. |
| **F** | Significant parts incomplete or major lack of clarity. |

## Writing Quality Standards

### Academic Voice
- Third person preferred (first person acceptable for methodological choices: "I propose to...")
- Hedging language for uncertain claims ("suggests," "indicates," "may contribute to")
- Define key concepts on first use
- British English spelling conventions (organisation, analyse, behaviour) — consistent with MAU/Swedish academic norms

### Problematization over Gap-Spotting
Following Sandberg & Alvesson (2011), a key course reading:
- **Gap-spotting** (weaker): "No study has examined X" — identifies an absence
- **Problematization** (stronger): Challenges assumptions in existing literature, questions taken-for-granted positions, reveals tensions between theories or between theory and practice
- Aim for problematization to achieve higher grades

### Flow and Coherence
The proposal must read as one integrated argument, not five disconnected sections:
- Background → identifies the problem and theoretical territory
- Previous studies → shows what has been done and what remains
- Methods → explains how to address what remains
- Practical contribution → shows why it matters beyond academia
- The **purpose statement** is the hinge connecting all sections

### Citation Style (APA 7)

| Situation | Format |
|-----------|--------|
| 1 author | (Author, Year) |
| 2 authors | (Author & Author, Year) |
| 3+ authors | (Author et al., Year) |
| Direct quote | (Author et al., Year, p. XX) |
| Narrative | Author et al. (Year) argue that... |
| Organisation (first) | (United Nations [UN], Year) |
| Organisation (subsequent) | (UN, Year) |

## Zotero Integration (Citation Helper)

When drafting the proposal, use the `/scholar:citation-helper` skill to search the researcher's Zotero library (the Zotero CSL-JSON library configured in the plugin's `.local.md` file (default: `references/salsu-library.json`)) for sources rather than inventing or web-searching for references.

### Citation Workflow During Drafting

1. **For each claim needing a citation:** Search the Zotero library first using `/scholar:citation-helper` (Operation 1 or 5)
2. **Insert Pandoc citation keys:** Use `[@citekey]` format (e.g., `[@sandberg.alvesson2011]`)
3. **Multiple sources:** `[@key1; @key2]`
4. **Narrative citations:** `@citekey` (e.g., `@sandberg.alvesson2011 argue that...`)

### Source Lookup Priority

```
0. ZOTERO: Search the Zotero CSL-JSON library configured in the plugin's .local.md file by author, title, keyword, year
   ├── Match found → Use [@citekey] — researcher already has this source
   └── No match ↓
1. LOCAL PDFs: Search the local PDFs directory configured in .local.md for relevant papers (especially course readings)
   └── No match ↓
2. WEB SEARCH: Search for additional academic sources (fallback)
```

Prioritise sources the researcher already has. Course readings in the local PDFs directory are particularly relevant for research methods proposals.

### Reference List Generation

After drafting, use `/scholar:citation-helper` Operation 4 to generate the APA 7 reference list from all `[@citekey]` citations in the document.

## Process

1. **Clarify the research topic:** Ask the user for their topic, SDG focus, and any theories they want to use. If they already have a draft, read it first.
2. **Develop the argument arc:** Problem → gap/tension in literature → purpose → questions → methods → contribution. Ensure logical flow.
3. **Search Zotero library:** Use `/scholar:citation-helper` to find relevant sources for the background, theories, and previous studies sections.
4. **Draft section by section** following the word targets, citing with `[@citekey]` format:
   - a) Background & problem: 500–750 words
   - b) Previous studies: ~500 words
   - c) Methods & RQs: ~500 words
   - d) Practical contribution: 250–500 words
5. **Generate reference list:** Use `/scholar:citation-helper` Operation 4 to build the reference list from cited keys.
6. **Verify alignment:** Check that RQs align with purpose, methods fit RQs, and contribution connects back to the problem.
7. **Check word count:** Total body must be 1,800–2,200 words (excluding references, figures, diagrams).
8. **Build DOCX** with professional formatting (or use Pandoc with `--citeproc` and the APA CSL file configured in `.local.md` for automatic citation rendering).
9. **Final check against grading criteria:** Originality, relevance, soundness, coherence, quality, feasibility.
