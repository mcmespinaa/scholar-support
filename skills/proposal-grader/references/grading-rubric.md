# OL652E Research Proposal -- Grading Rubric

Source: Hug, S.E., & Aeschbach, M. (2020). Criteria for assessing grant applications: a systematic review. *Palgrave Communications*, 6, 37. https://doi.org/10.1057/s41599-020-0412-9

## Hug & Aeschbach Framework: How It Works

The systematic review (12 studies, qualitative content analysis) identifies that what is commonly called a "criterion" actually has two components:

1. **Evaluated entity** -- the *object* being assessed (e.g., the research idea, the proposed methods, the research plan)
2. **Evaluation criterion** -- the *dimension* along which the entity is assessed (e.g., originality, clarity, feasibility)

The paper identifies **15 evaluation criteria** applied across **30 evaluated entities**. Gustav's rubric selects the most relevant criteria and maps them to the OL652E assignment structure:

### How Entities and Criteria Map to the Proposal

| Evaluated entity | Evaluation criteria applied | Proposal section |
|-----------------|---------------------------|-----------------|
| Research idea / aims / outcomes | Originality, Academic relevance, Extra-academic relevance | (a) Background |
| Proposed research process | Quality (rigor), Appropriateness, Coherence/justification | (c) Methods |
| Description of the proposal | Clarity, Completeness | All sections |
| Resources needed for implementation | Feasibility | (c) Methods, research plan |
| Link between problem and questions | Coherence | (a) Background ↔ (c) RQs |
| Link between methods and questions | Appropriateness | (c) Methods ↔ (c) RQs |

### Key Insight: Evaluation Is Multi-Layered

A single proposal section is evaluated on multiple criteria simultaneously. For example, the methods section is assessed for:
- **Appropriateness** -- do the methods fit the questions?
- **Quality/rigor** -- are the methods well-designed?
- **Clarity** -- is the methods description understandable?
- **Feasibility** -- can these methods realistically be executed?

A strong methods section must score well on ALL of these, not just one.

## Assessment Dimensions (Applied to OL652E)

### Originality
The research idea should be novel and ideally engage with the research frontier -- not merely replicate existing work. Hug & Aeschbach note that originality is assessed specifically on the *aims and outcomes* of the proposed project, not on the methods or description. A well-known method applied to a novel question is still original; a novel method applied to a settled question is not.

### Academic and Extra-Academic Relevance
- **Scientific value (academic relevance)**: Does the proposed research contribute to scholarly understanding? Is the contribution to the field clearly articulated?
- **Societal value (extra-academic relevance)**: Does this address real-world problems, SDG targets, or stakeholder needs?

Hug & Aeschbach treat these as equally weighted -- societal relevance is not a secondary add-on to academic relevance. In the OL652E context, the SDG connection and practical contribution (section d) carry real grading weight.

### Plausibility and Soundness
This criterion applies to the *research process* (not the idea). It has three sub-dimensions:
- **Coherence/justification**: Do the parts fit together logically? Is there an unbroken argument chain from problem to purpose to questions to methods?
- **Appropriateness**: Are the chosen theories and methods suitable for the research problem and questions?
- **Quality/rigor**: Is the proposed research process methodologically sound?

### Background ↔ Questions Alignment
There must be a clear and appropriate link between the research background/problem and the research aims/questions. This is a *coherence* assessment: does the background logically necessitate the stated questions, or do the questions feel disconnected from the problem discussion?

### Methods ↔ Questions Alignment
There must be a clear and appropriate link between the research methods and the research aims/questions. This is an *appropriateness* assessment: could the proposed methods actually answer the stated questions?

For OL652E specifically: the rubric requires 2 qualitative RQs + 2 quantitative RQs, each with corresponding methods. The grader must check that qualitative methods match qualitative RQs and quantitative methods match quantitative RQs.

### Quality of Description
- **Clarity**: Is the writing clear, precise, and well-structured?
- **Completeness**: Are all required elements present and developed?

Hug & Aeschbach emphasise that this criterion applies to *the description itself*, not to the underlying ideas. A brilliant research idea poorly described scores low on this dimension but may score high on originality. The grader must distinguish between weak ideas and weak writing.

### Feasibility
The author must demonstrate awareness of the resources needed:
- Is the research process logical and feasible?
- Is data available/accessible?
- Is the research environment appropriate?
- Does the author have (or can develop) the necessary abilities?

Hug & Aeschbach's key finding: feasibility is assessed on the *resources needed to implement the research process*. An overambitious design (e.g., a full mixed-methods study with 18 interviews and 200 survey respondents for a master's thesis) actively scores lower than a simpler design that the researcher can realistically execute. Feasibility is not a checkbox -- it is a substantive assessment criterion.

## Strategic Implications for Grading

1. **Coherence beats complexity.** A simple, well-aligned proposal (clear problem -> clear purpose -> matching RQs -> appropriate methods) scores higher than an ambitious but fragmented one.
2. **Feasibility penalises overreach.** Proposing more than can be achieved does not demonstrate ambition -- it demonstrates lack of resource awareness.
3. **Section (c) tests methodological literacy, not commitment.** The rubric asks students to show they can think from both perspectives, using conditional language ("this could be studied..."). It does NOT ask for a mixed-methods design.
4. **Section (b) must review articles by method.** The rubric specifies: review articles "in terms of their research questions, scope of study, methods, data used and results" -- not just thematic grouping. Balance between qualitative and quantitative studies is explicitly requested.
5. **Extra-academic relevance carries equal weight.** SDG links and stakeholder value are not decorative -- they are graded with the same weight as academic contribution.

## Grade Descriptors

### Grade A (Excellent)
The research idea is original and engages with the research frontier. It excellently addresses academic and extra-academic relevance, and has an exceptional flow between the background, purpose, and research questions. The relevance between the concepts/theories, questions, and methods is excellent in the proposal. The text has almost no flaws and all criteria for grade B are superseded.

### Grade B (Very Good)
The research idea is original and well-explains the academic and extra-academic relevance, the background, purpose, and questions. The relevance between the concepts/theories, questions, and methods is well-illustrated in the proposal. The text is very well-structured. Plus, all criteria for grade C.

### Grade C (Good)
- There is a clear and appropriate link between research background/problem and the research purpose/questions.
- The text shows that the author is aware of the resources needed to implement the project (e.g., research plan is logical and feasible, research environment, data availability, researchers' abilities).
- The text indicates a fair understanding of how to formulate research questions, relevant research methods, data gathering methods, and data analysis methods, and the clarity needed in a research proposal.
- The author has cited the relevant literature about the concepts/theories used in the proposal and the research methods.
- The proposal includes all sections asked in the assignment and is well-structured.

### Grade D–E (Satisfactory / Pass)
- The research idea is relevant and clear.
- The proposal includes key sections of a research proposal.
- The text indicates that the student has achieved the course's learning goals.

### Grade F (Fail)
Significant parts of the assignment have not been completed, or a major part of the report lacks clarity.
